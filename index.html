Section 5 · Reading Passage 1

How Houses Have Changed Through Time

Long before history was written, human beings needed protection from the weather, wild animals and other dangers. The first people climbed into trees or hid inside natural caves. Later, in the Stone Age, they used simple tools to put branches on top of one another and to stretch animal skins over wooden frames. Step by step, they discovered that they could shape clay into bricks, dry the bricks in the sun, and use them to construct stronger walls.

In the great ancient civilisations of Egypt, Greece and Rome, architects designed houses that depended on the local climate. In hot regions, walls were thick to keep rooms cool, and roofs were flat. In rainy areas, roofs were sloped so the water could slide off. Some Roman houses even had an early form of central heating: hot air passed through pipes under the floor.

During the European Middle Ages, ordinary people lived in dark, one-room houses with small windows and no glass. The wealthy, however, built fortified castles with thick stone walls. The main purpose of a castle was security — for the family, the workers, the animals and the food stores.

The Industrial Revolution of the 18th and 19th centuries changed everything. Factories manufactured cheaper bricks, glass and metal. Architects could now design with three goals in mind: durability, practicality and beauty. Today we live in a wide range of homes — from small apartments to huge mansions — and tomorrow’s homes may even produce their own electricity from the sun.

Section 6 · Reading Passage 2

The Family Through the Centuries

The word family has not always meant the same thing. In prehistoric times, a family was simply a group of people who shared the same shelter and worked together to survive. Their main duty was to protect their offspring so the human race could continue.

In ancient Roman society, the father was the head of the household, which often included his wife, his grown-up children and several servants. Later, in pre-industrial Europe, two main forms appeared: the nuclear family (parents and their children) and the extended family (more than two generations living together). A family’s size depended on how long its members lived; its social class depended on the property it owned.

The Industrial Revolution changed family life completely. Because adults had to work long hours in factories, governments and schools began to share some of the parents’ duties. As a result, children spent more time outside the home and developed stronger links with society.

In modern times there is no single ‘correct’ family form. Some people reside alone, others live as a couple without children, and many still belong to large extended families. Whatever the form, however, the family remains closely connected to the idea of home — a safe place where people are loved and guided.
